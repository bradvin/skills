# Meta Description Pattern Library

Meta descriptions don't affect rankings — they affect CTR only. Think of them as
ad copy for the SERP. The job is to answer the emotional question behind the search
query and give a specific reason to click over the other 9 results.

---

## The anatomy of a high-CTR meta description

1. **Hook** — Address the core desire or anxiety behind the search (first 80 chars,
   visible on mobile before truncation)
2. **Proof** — A specific claim that creates trust or sets expectations
3. **CTA** — An action or forward-looking phrase that creates mild urgency

All three don't need to be explicit — but the best metas hit at least two.

---

## Pattern 1: Validate + Specifics + CTA
Structure: `[Validates the desire]. [Specific proof — numbers, platforms, comparisons]. [CTA]`
Example: `Thousands of photographers earn steady income selling photos online. Here's exactly how: the best platforms, what each pays, and how to get started today.`
Best for: Informational guides where the user's main concern is "does this actually work?"
Hook: Social proof ("thousands of photographers")
Proof: Specificity ("best platforms, what each pays")
CTA: Urgency ("today")

## Pattern 2: Problem + Solution + Specificity
Structure: `[State the problem]. [We solved it — here's the evidence]`
Example: `Still wondering which platform to use to sell your photos? We compared 10 sites on payout, ease of use, and control — so you don't have to.`
Best for: Comparison intent queries where the user is in research mode
Hook: Empathy (acknowledges the confusion)
Proof: Specificity (10 sites, three criteria)
CTA: Implicit ("so you don't have to" = save time)

## Pattern 3: Direct outcome
Structure: `[Primary keyword] can earn you [specific outcome]. Here's [what the guide covers].`
Example: `Selling photos online can earn you a few hundred dollars to a full income, depending on your approach. This guide covers every realistic path.`
Best for: Monetization queries where range and realism builds more trust than hype
Hook: Specificity of outcome range (more credible than "make thousands!")
Proof: "every realistic path" = comprehensive
CTA: Implicit

## Pattern 4: Emotional mirror
Structure: `[Reflects what the user is feeling/thinking]. [Here's what you'll get]`
Example: `Those photos sitting on your hard drive could be earning money right now. Find out which platforms pay best and how to start in under an hour.`
Best for: Queries from people who've been meaning to act but haven't yet
Hook: Very specific emotional image (hard drive photos)
Proof: "which platforms pay best"
CTA: Time specificity ("under an hour")

## Pattern 5: Comparison teaser
Structure: `[N] platforms for selling photos online, compared on [criteria]. [Outcome].`
Example: `10 platforms for selling photos online, ranked by payout rate, ease of use, and how much control you keep. Find the right fit for your photography.`
Best for: Comparison queries where users want help deciding
Hook: Number specificity
Proof: Three named criteria (structured, credible)
CTA: "Find the right fit" = personalized value

---

## Meta Description Rules

**Length**: 140–155 characters is ideal. Google truncates at ~920px (~155–160 chars).
Shorter is acceptable if the hook is strong. Never pad to hit a length target.

**Mobile first**: The first ~80 characters appear in mobile previews. Put the emotional
hook or primary value proposition in the first half.

**Include the primary keyword**: Google will bold it in the SERP snippet when it matches
the search query — more visual weight, higher trust signal. Fit it naturally, don't
force it.

**Avoid**: Generic openings ("In this article, we will..."), passive voice, vague claims
("comprehensive guide," "everything you need to know" without specifics), duplicate
meta descriptions across pages (each page needs a unique meta).

**Do not copy the title**: The meta should add new information — expand on the benefit,
add the emotional hook, or explain what the content specifically contains.

**Active voice, present tense**: "We compare 10 platforms" reads more directly than
"10 platforms are compared." Present tense feels current.

**Test on a SERP snippet previewer before shipping**: Yoast, RankMath, and
`metatags.io` all show a realistic preview. Confirm nothing is truncated at a
weird point.
