# Title Tag Pattern Library

High-CTR title structures with examples. Use these as frameworks, not templates —
always fit to the specific keyword, content, and competitive landscape.

---

## Pattern 1: How-to + Number + Outcome
Structure: `How to [Primary Keyword]: [N] [Things] That [Benefit]`
Example: `How to Sell Photos Online: 10 Platforms That Pay`
Best for: Instructional intent queries with list-format content
CTR mechanism: Specificity (number) + outcome promise
Watch out: Only use a number if the content actually has that many items

## Pattern 2: Year-Tagged Guide
Structure: `How to [Primary Keyword] in [Year]: [Differentiator]`
Example: `How to Sell Photos Online in 2026 — A Complete Platform Guide`
Best for: Competitive niches where freshness signals matter
CTR mechanism: Recency / trust
Watch out: Requires annual update to stay credible; becomes dated quickly

## Pattern 3: Outcome-First
Structure: `[Desired Outcome]: How to [Primary Keyword]`
Example: `Make Money Selling Photos Online: The 2026 Photographer's Guide`
Best for: Monetization / side-hustle queries where the outcome is the point
CTR mechanism: Benefit-first / emotional hook
Watch out: Can feel like clickbait in professional B2B contexts

## Pattern 4: Specificity + Comparison
Structure: `[Primary Keyword]: [N] [Things] Compared`
Example: `Sell Photos Online: 12 Platforms Compared for Photographers`
Best for: Queries with commercial comparison intent
CTR mechanism: Specificity + saves-time promise
Watch out: Implies comprehensive comparison — content must deliver

## Pattern 5: Question Format
Structure: `[Question searchers ask]? [Short answer preview]`
Example: `Can You Really Make Money Selling Photos Online? Yes — Here's How`
Best for: Queries driven by doubt or skepticism
CTR mechanism: Curiosity + validation
Watch out: Question mark in title tag can look informal; avoid for B2B

## Pattern 6: Direct + Confident
Structure: `[Primary Keyword]: [Confident declarative claim]`
Example: `Sell Photos Online and Keep More of What You Earn`
Best for: Competitive niches where differentiation is on value, not just information
CTR mechanism: Benefit specificity + implied contrast with competitors
Watch out: Weaker keyword match; only works when content is clearly differentiated

## Pattern 7: Audience-Targeted
Structure: `How to [Primary Keyword] for [Specific Audience]`
Example: `How to Sell Photos Online for Amateur Photographers (2026)`
Best for: Generic queries where most content targets professionals
CTR mechanism: Relevance / specificity / "this is for me"
Watch out: Narrows the audience — only use if the content genuinely serves that segment

## Title Tag Rules

**Length**: 50–60 characters is optimal (Google typically truncates at ~580px / ~60 chars).
Anything 61–70 chars may truncate on mobile. Check actual pixel width, not just character
count — wide characters (W, M) count more than narrow ones (i, l).

**Primary keyword placement**: Place the primary keyword as close to the start as possible.
Google bolds keyword matches — front-loading gets more visual weight.

**Pipe vs dash vs colon**:
- Pipe `|` is clean but reads as a divider — good for brand suffixes (`Page Title | Brand`)
- Em dash `—` reads as "here's more" — good for adding a benefit or detail
- Colon `:` reads as "specifically" — good for subtitle-style additions
- Avoid using more than one separator in a single title

**Brand suffix**: WordPress SEO plugins (Yoast, RankMath) typically auto-append `| Brand`.
If present, it eats 12–18 characters. Factor this into length planning. For non-branded
queries, the brand suffix often hurts CTR — consider disabling it on informational pages
where the brand is not the draw.

**Numbers**: Use numerals (10, not ten) — they're more scannable in a SERP and take
fewer characters. Numbers in titles have consistently higher CTR in controlled tests.

**Year tags**: Include the year for guides, comparisons, and any "best X" content.
Don't include it for evergreen reference pages where dating would be a disadvantage.

**Avoid**: ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation (!!), vague words (Ultimate, Amazing, Best
without specificity), misleading claims that content doesn't support.
